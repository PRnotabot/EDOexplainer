The Motivation

Why Optimization Matters

Small design changes can yield dramatic performance gains — but only if we search systematically.

Aerodynamics

Optimized airfoil shapes reduce drag by 25–75%

Structures

Topology optimization cuts weight by 40–60%

Turbines

Blade profile tuning boosts efficiency by 2–5%

“The goal of engineering design optimization is to find the best design as measured by one or more quantities of interest, while satisfying all requirements and constraints.”

— Martins & Ning, Engineering Design Optimization, 2022

Design optimization replaces trial-and-error with mathematics.

The Optimization Problem

Every design optimization has the same structure:

minimize   f(x)

subject to   g(x) ≤ 0

                xL ≤ x ≤ xU

Design Variables x

Shape coordinates, material thicknesses, angles — what we control

Objective f(x)

Drag, weight, fuel burn, compliance — what we minimize

Constraints g(x)

Stress limits, lift requirements, volume bounds

[Martins & Ning, Ch. 1, 2022]

What Makes Design Optimization Hard?

Computational Cost

Each evaluation requires solving complex physics (CFD, FEA). A single simulation can take hours to days.

High Dimensionality

Real designs have hundreds to thousands of variables. Exhaustive search is impossible.

Coupled Disciplines

Aerodynamics, structures, and controls interact. Optimizing one can worsen another.

These three challenges motivate everything that follows: gradient-based methods, the adjoint method, and multidisciplinary optimization.

The Central Example

The Wing Design Problem

A wing must balance aerodynamic and structural objectives that directly conflict.

Aerodynamics wants a thin, high-aspect-ratio wing → less drag

Structures wants a thick, short wing → less weight and stress

“The wing is perhaps the most important component of an aircraft, and its design involves a fundamental tradeoff between aerodynamic efficiency and structural weight.”

— Martins & Ning, Ch. 13, 2022

The best wing is one that optimizes the coupled system — a multidisciplinary design optimization problem.

The design space is vast and expensive to explore.

How do we navigate it efficiently?

Shape Parametrization

NURBS Curves

Design variables need a parametrization — a way to define shapes with a finite set of numbers.

NURBS (Non-Uniform Rational B-Splines) use control points that shape the curve without touching it.

C(t) = Σ Ni,p(t) · Pi

Each control point has local influence — ideal for optimization. Drag the cyan points to see!

Gradient-Based Optimization

The Optimization Loop

Search Direction

Steepest Descent

The gradient ∇f points in the direction of steepest increase.

So we move in the opposite direction:

p = −∇f

Simple and intuitive — but it has a problem.

Watch Gradient Descent Navigate

Click anywhere to set a starting point. Observe the zigzag path of steepest descent on elongated contours.

Smarter Optimizers

Steepest descent ignores curvature. Better methods exploit it.

Quasi-Newton (BFGS)

Approximates the Hessian from gradient history. Converges superlinearly.

SQP (Constrained)

Solves quadratic subproblems respecting linearized constraints. Handles g(x) ≤ 0.

“BFGS is widely regarded as the most effective quasi-Newton method for general unconstrained optimization.”

— Nocedal & Wright, Numerical Optimization, 2006

BFGS and SQP (e.g., SNOPT) are the workhorses of gradient-based design optimization.

Every Method Needs Gradients

Steepest descent, BFGS, SQP — they all require:

∇f = [ ∂f/∂x1, ∂f/∂x2, ..., ∂f/∂xn ]

In design optimization, n can be hundreds or thousands of design variables.

How do we compute these gradients efficiently?

The optimizer knows how to navigate.

But it needs a compass — the gradient.

Computing this compass is the real challenge.

Method 1

Finite Differences

The simplest approach: perturb each variable and re-evaluate.

∂f/∂xi ≈ [f(x + hêi) − f(x)] / h

Problem: needs one function evaluation per design variable.

500 design variables = 501 physics solves

[Martins & Ning, Ch. 6, 2022]

Finite differences are useful for verifying analytic gradients, but too expensive for production optimization.

The Physics

The Implicit Model

Engineering analysis involves solving governing equations:

r(u; x) = 0

x = design variables (shape, thickness, angle)
u = state variables (pressure, velocity, displacement)
r = residual equations (Navier–Stokes, structural equilibrium)

For a wing: x = airfoil shape + skin thickness, u = flow field + structural deflection, f = drag or fuel burn.

Total Derivative via Chain Rule

Because f depends on x both directly and through u:

df/dx = ∂f/∂x + (∂f/∂u)(du/dx)

∂f/∂x

Direct effect. Cheap to compute.

∂f/∂u

Sensitivity of f to states. Also cheap.

du/dx

How states change with design. Expensive!

The challenge is computing du/dx without re-solving the nonlinear equations nx times.

Differentiating the Governing Equations

Since r(u; x) = 0 must hold, differentiating with respect to x gives:

(∂r/∂u) (du/dx) = −(∂r/∂x)

∂r/∂u

Square matrix (nu × nu). We already have this from the solver!

∂r/∂x

Matrix (nu × nx). Cheap partial derivatives.

A linear system — much cheaper than re-solving the original nonlinear equations!

Forward Mode

The Direct Method

Solve the linear system once per design variable:

(∂r/∂u) φi = ∂r/∂xi

for i = 1, 2, ..., nx

Then compute the total derivative:

df/dx = ∂f/∂x − (∂f/∂u) φ

Cost: nx linear solves
For 500 design variables = 500 solves

The Key Insight

The Adjoint Method

Instead of solving column by column (one per design variable)...

...solve row by row (one per function of interest):

(∂r/∂u)T ψ = (∂f/∂u)T

Then compute all derivatives at once:

df/dx = ∂f/∂x − ψT (∂r/∂x)

Cost: nf linear solves
For 1 objective = 1 solve (regardless of number of design variables!)

“The adjoint approach was first applied to shape optimization by Pironneau (1974) and brought to computational aerodynamics by Jameson (1988).”

— Martins & Ning, 2022

One Solve → All Gradients

Algorithmic Differentiation

Instead of deriving adjoint equations by hand, AD differentiates the code itself.

Forward Mode (Tangent)

Propagates derivatives with the computation. Cost ∝ nx.

Analogous to the direct method.

Reverse Mode (Adjoint)

Propagates derivatives backward. Cost ∝ nf.

Analogous to the adjoint method — and to backpropagation!

Reverse-mode AD is the same algorithm as backpropagation in deep learning — applied to physics simulations.

[Griewank & Walther, Evaluating Derivatives, 2008]

Unified Derivative Equations

All derivative methods are special cases of one framework.

Method Seed Solves Best When
Finite Differences eᵢ nx + 1 Verification
Direct / Forward AD eᵢ nx nx < nf
Adjoint / Reverse AD eⱼ nf nf < nx

“The unified derivatives equations provide a single mathematical framework that encompasses finite differences, the direct method, the adjoint method, and algorithmic differentiation.”

— Martins & Hwang, AIAA Journal, 2013

For design optimization (nx >> nf), the adjoint/reverse mode is always the right choice.

We can efficiently compute gradients for any single discipline.

But real problems have coupled disciplines.

Beyond Single Disciplines

Multidisciplinary Design Optimization

MDO optimizes systems where multiple disciplines interact — finding designs that no single-discipline approach could.

“MDO is a field of research that studies the application of numerical optimization techniques to the design of engineering systems involving multiple interacting disciplines.”

— Martins & Lambe, Struct. Multidisc. Optim., 2013

Optimizing the system yields better results than optimizing each discipline separately and compromising.

XDSM: Extended Design Structure Matrix

A visual language for describing MDO processes.

[Lambe & Martins, Struct. Multidisc. Optim., 2012]

[Lambe & Martins, 2012]

XDSM: Single-Discipline Optimization

The simplest case: one optimizer, one analysis.

XDSM: Coupled Analysis (MDA)

When disciplines are coupled, they must iterate to consistency.

MDF: Multidisciplinary Feasible

The most common MDO architecture: the optimizer drives an inner MDA loop.

“MDF is the most straightforward MDO architecture. The optimizer sees a consistent multidisciplinary system at every iteration.”

— Martins & Lambe, 2013

Pros

Physics always consistent. Off-the-shelf optimizers work. Robust.

Cons

MDA must converge at every optimization iteration. Can be expensive.

MDF guarantees multidisciplinary feasibility at every step — the safest approach for coupled problems.

The Coupled Adjoint

For MDO, the adjoint equations become coupled across disciplines:

[ ∂RA/∂uA ∂RA/∂uS ] ψ = (∂f/∂u)ᵀ
[ ∂RS/∂uA ∂RS/∂uS ]ᵀ

“The coupled adjoint method extends the efficiency of the adjoint approach to multidisciplinary systems, enabling gradient computation at a cost independent of the number of design variables.”

Key Takeaways

  • Gradient-based optimization is essential for high-dimensional engineering design spaces. BFGS and SQP are the workhorses.
  • The adjoint method is the key enabler. It computes all gradients at a cost independent of the number of design variables.
  • Algorithmic differentiation automates derivatives. Forward and reverse modes map directly to direct and adjoint methods.
  • MDO optimizes the coupled system. Aerostructural optimization finds designs that sequential approaches miss entirely.
  • The coupled adjoint scales MDO. Hundreds of design variables across multiple disciplines, all gradients from one coupled solve.
